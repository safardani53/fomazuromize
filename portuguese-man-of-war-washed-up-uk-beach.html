Dozens of killer Portuguese Man-of-war found washed up on UK beach as bizarre invasion continues

 Posted on July 11, 2024  |  3 minutes  |  548 words  |  Aldo Pusey

NEW pictures have revealed the extent of Britain's "unprecedented" Portuguese Man O'War invasion - as the deadly creatures wash onto Cornwall's beaches in record numbers.

Horrified surfers on Cornwall's Fistral beach were forced to spend their Sunday stepping around the deadly purple and blue creatures, which were carried to British waters by strong westerly winds from the mid-Atlantic.

The Cornwall Wildlife Trust said there had been 144 sightings of the lethal marine animal - known as the floating terror - in September alone, beating the previous record of 40 across the whole of 2009.

Perranporth beach in north Cornwall was temporary closed as a precaution due to the numbers, while marine experts warned parents were warned to keep kids away from the balloon-like critters.

Mum Maggie Sloggett said she couldn’t believe her eyes when she spotted five of the animals along the beach as she walked along the coast with her teen daughter.

The Cornwall local told the Sun Online she had been walking along the Greenaway beach on Saturday afternoon with her daughter when they realised the tide had brought in the creatures.

She told The Sun Online: “First of all I thought it was a plastic bottle or a balloon but we walked closer to it and realised what we were looking at.”

She said some of the creatures were as big as a Cornish pasty.

The mum said: “I was with my daughter who is 16 and I felt quite safe, but if I had my younger children with me, I would have been quite worried.”

She added: “They were all in quite a close vicinity to each other – we would see one, walk for about 100 yards and we saw another. “It’s a danger.”

The flood comes just weeks after authorities in Ireland warned locals about a spate of recent sightings of the potentially lethal creature.

Despite popular misconception, Portuguese Men O'War are not technically jellyfish but collections of individual animals which work together in a balloon-like float.

Their stings cause severe pain to humans and leave bright red welts on the skin for days afterwards - and in some cases can be fatal.

In 2010, 69-year-old Maria Furcas died after being stung on the leg by one of the creatures while swimming at a resort in Sardinia.

Paramedics were unable to save her after she told a lifeguard she had been stung and then collapsed from anaphylactic shock.

Matt Slater, Marine Awareness Officer for Cornwall Wildlife Trust, said: "This is an unprecedented event and we urge the public to be cautious and to keep an eye out for unusual species being stranded."

Dr Peter Richardson, head of ocean recovery at the Marine Conservation Society, added: "Because a stranded Portuguese Man O'War looks a bit like a deflating purple balloon with blue ribbons attached, children will find it fascinating.

"So, if you're visiting west coast beaches in the next few weeks it's well worth making sure you know what these animals look like and that no one picks them up.

"The stings can be unbelievably painful and in rare cases, fatal."
