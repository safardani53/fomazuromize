For those who don't know, the first Jefferson design was engraved by Felix Schlag and released into circulation in 1938.

Then a redesign of the nickel was produced in 2004 to honor the 200th anniversary of the Louisiana Purchase, which was authorized by the third US President.

The new nickel has a portrait of Mr Jefferson on the obverse, while the reverse depicts a Monticello, the home of Thomas Jefferson.

The particular Jefferson nickel in the recent listing features the date 1975 without a mintmark.

But most importantly, there is embedded copper error on the reverse, making it valuable.

How to spot valuable coins

A way to check your nickel's worth is by analyzing if there's a mint mark on the coin.

A mintmark features a letter that represents where the coin was made.

Common letter marks include an S or a D, which stand for San Fransisco and Denver, respectively.

Coins minted in Philadelphia will typically either feature a "P" or no letter mark.

Letter marks on Jefferson nickels could be found near the top of the reverse (tails) or on the bottom right of the obverse (heads).

Also, collectors typically look for coins with low mintages as they tend to be worth more.

Additionally, you'll want to look out for unique silver alloy nickels, which were minted between 1942 and 1945.

Because they contain silver, each is a premium value nickel.

Large mint marks were placed on all of the silver alloy nickels to indicate the alloy change.

This means the "S", "D, or "P marks will be printed more boldly above the Monticello picture.

Typically, the most valuable coins are ones with a low mintage or an error, as collectors find those coins are some of the rarest.
